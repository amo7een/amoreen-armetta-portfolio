Case study · AI-assisted content operations

Designing and implementing a content operations system

I mapped the documentation lifecycle and designed a system that combined AI agents and human judgment.

Role Senior Technical Writer

Focus Content operations, workflow design, AI assistance

Impact Approximately three hours saved per writer per day on a three-person team

01

The problem

Product info was scattered

The team spent significant time gathering context before they could scope or draft documentation.

Engineering tickets
Slack conversations
Product briefs
Design specs
Meeting notes
Support feedback
↓

Manual search

When writers understood the scope of the feature updates, they strategized how to fit the changes into the existing documentation.

  • What changed?
  • Which articles were affected?
  • What needed to be created or updated?
  • How did this affect the Help Center architecture as a whole?

02

The system

AI agents supported the work. Writers made the decisions.

The agents surfaced documentation needs, generated starting points, and flagged potential quality issues. Writers remained responsible for product accuracy, structure, voice, final approval, and manual publishing.

Product signals

Search product briefs, engineering work, conversations, and release information

↓
Documentation detection

Identify product changes and content likely to be affected

↓
Content analysis

Find gaps, outdated guidance, and conflicting information

↓
Draft and editorial review

Generate a starting point, then verify, rewrite, and refine

↓
Quality checks

Flag terminology, accessibility, style, and contradiction risks for writer review

↓
Publish and maintain

Release approved content and surface future maintenance needs

03

Human-in-the-loop design

The most important decision was where not to automate

AI agent assistance

  • Collect product context
  • Detect possible content gaps
  • Generate initial drafts
  • Flag accessibility issues
  • Identify possible contradictions

Human judgment

  • Verify product behavior
  • Determine user needs
  • Choose article structure
  • Rewrite for clarity and context
  • Check terminology and style
  • Verify accuracy with PM or engineer
  • Ensure article fits with overall content strategy

04

The result

A faster, more consistent documentation operation

Time saved ~3 hours saved per writer per day on a 3-person team
Earlier visibility into upcoming product changes
More consistent terminology, style, and accessibility
Increased capacity for gathering metrics, content strategy, and help center design

How the time saved was measured: Based on ticket time-tracking data from the three-person writing team, which showed roughly three hours a day spent searching engineering roadmaps and backlogs, Slack conversations, ticket comments, and product briefs, and asking subject matter experts questions, to understand exactly what was being updated.

Pricing and packaging

Building a reusable content system

Some documentation problems require changing how the information is organized.

Problem

Plan, pricing, and permission details were repeated across dozens of help center articles. Each product change required multiple manual updates.

Approach

Designed a custom reusable content system so availability and limits information could be defined once and referenced across every article in the feature group, rather than duplicated in each article.

Built using custom CSS in Zendesk, styled to match ClickUp's brand colors.

Result

Pricing and packaging changes required one update instead of dozens, reducing maintenance work and content drift.
